How an Autonomous Decision Controller (ADC) Thinks

A plain-English guide to the 7 alerts that power real intelligence

BM
Boris Mizhen
February 2026 • 10 min read

Most software today can report what happened. Some can predict what might happen.

Very few systems can safely decide and act.

An Autonomous Decision Controller (ADC) is designed to do exactly that — by thinking through decisions the same way a disciplined human expert would, but faster, more consistently, and with perfect memory.

This article explains the 7 alerts or stages of an ADC in simple terms, and how they work together to create a true, traceable decision engine.

The Big Idea

Think of the ADC as a highly trained decision professional that never sleeps. It notices what is happening, understands what it means, plans possible responses, checks itself for mistakes, chooses carefully, acts safely, and learns permanently.

This is the opposite of black-box AI.

SENSE→REASON→PLAN→VALIDATE→DECIDE→ACT→LEARN

The 7 ADC Alerts

Alert 1
SENSE Alert
“Something changed. Pay attention.”

The system notices something unusual, important, or new: a sudden drop in performance, a spike in customer interest, a change in market conditions, or a signal disappearing due to privacy limits.

Why this is genius: The ADC records the signal without panicking.

Alert 2
REASON Alert
“What is actually going on?”

The system asks whether the pattern is real or random, whether it has happened before, and what past experience says. This is understanding, not authority.

Alert 3
PLAN Alert
“What could we do about it?”

The ADC creates options: increase, reduce, wait, test, or do nothing. No action is taken yet.

Alert 4
VALIDATE Alert
“Would this action actually cause the outcome we want?”

This is causal reasoning, not guesswork. If causation cannot be established, the system refuses to act automatically.

This is where safety lives.

Alert 5
DECIDE Alert
“Are we justified in acting?”

Only after validation does the ADC decide whether to recommend, require approval, or execute autonomously. Every decision is policy-checked and logged.

Alert 6
ACT Alert
“We are acting — carefully.”

If all prior checks are satisfied, the ADC executes with bounded, reversible, monitored action.

Alert 7
LEARN Alert
“What did we learn from this?”

The system measures outcomes and writes learning into the Knowledge Graph so future decisions become smarter.

This is how intelligence compounds.

How the Rest of the Software Fits In

The Knowledge Graph is the system’s long-term memory. It stores observations, causal links, decisions, and outcomes so every decision can be explained, audited, and improved.

Final Takeaway

You are not looking at AI software. You are looking at a decision professional with perfect memory, built-in discipline, and the ability to explain itself.

Others predict behavior. The ADC governs decisions.
